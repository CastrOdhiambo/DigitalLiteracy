Section 6.1

What Is the Internet? — Key Terms & Concepts

What Is the Internet?

The Internet is a massive global network of interconnected computers, servers, phones, and devices that communicate with each other using a set of agreed rules (called protocols). It is not owned by any single person, company, or government — it is a shared global resource.

In simple terms: imagine millions of roads connecting millions of towns. The internet is those roads, and the information travelling on it is like cars and trucks moving from one place to another at incredible speed.

What Can You Do on the Internet?

Information & Learning
  • Search for any topic using Google
  • Watch educational videos on YouTube
  • Take online courses (free and paid)
  • Read news from around the world
  • Access digital books and research papers
Communication & Work
  • Send and receive emails instantly
  • Make video calls (WhatsApp, Zoom, Google Meet)
  • Apply for jobs and send CVs online
  • Access government services (eCitizen, KRA)
  • Run a business and reach customers

Essential Internet Glossary

Learn these terms — you will hear them every day when working with the internet.

Website

A collection of related web pages available under one address. Like a building with many rooms.

Example: www.google.com, www.ecitizen.go.ke
Web Page

A single document within a website — one screen of content you can read and interact with.

Example: The "About Us" page on a company website
Web Browser

Software you use to access and view websites. It reads website code and displays it visually.

Examples: Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, Mozilla Firefox
URL
Uniform Resource Locator

The unique address of every web page — like a postal address for a website.

Example: https://www.kra.go.ke/individual/pin-registration
Search Engine

A website that searches the entire internet for you and shows relevant results in seconds.

Examples: Google, Bing, Yahoo
HTTPS
HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure

The "S" means Secure — data between you and the website is encrypted. Always look for this.

✅ https://ecitizen.go.ke   ❌ http://ecitizen.go.ke
Download

Transferring a file FROM the internet TO your computer. You receive the file.

Example: Downloading a KRA PIN certificate PDF
Upload

Transferring a file FROM your computer TO the internet. You send the file.

Example: Uploading your ID scan to eCitizen
Bandwidth

The speed of your internet connection — how much data can flow per second. Measured in Mbps.

Higher Mbps = faster internet
IP Address
Internet Protocol Address

A unique number given to every device on the internet — like a digital postal address for your computer.

Example: 192.168.1.1
Wi-Fi

Wireless internet — connects your device to the internet through radio waves without a cable.

Most cybercafés, homes, and offices use Wi-Fi
Cookie

Small data files websites save on your computer to remember your preferences and login status.

Why websites remember your username automatically

Section 6.2

Connecting to the Internet

Method 1 — Connecting via Wi-Fi

Connect to Wi-Fi on Windows
  1. 1 Look at the bottom-right corner of the screen — click the Wi-Fi icon (🛜)
  2. 2 A list of available Wi-Fi networks appears — click the one you want
  3. 3 Click Connect
  4. 4 If the network is password-protected, a box appears — type the Wi-Fi password
  5. 5 Click Next
  6. 6 Wait until it shows "Connected" — you now have internet access
How to Tell You Are Connected

The Wi-Fi icon in the taskbar changes from grey bars to solid white/coloured bars. Open a browser and type google.com — if the page loads, you are connected.

Method 2 — Mobile Hotspot (Using Your Phone)

When no Wi-Fi is available, you can share your phone's mobile data as a personal hotspot. The phone becomes a mini router.

Android Phone Setup
  1. Settings → Connections
  2. Tap Mobile Hotspot and Tethering
  3. Tap Mobile Hotspot → toggle ON
  4. Note the hotspot name and password shown
Connect Computer to Hotspot
  1. Click the Wi-Fi icon on your computer
  2. Find your phone's hotspot name in the list
  3. Click it → click Connect
  4. Enter the hotspot password → Next
Hotspot Uses Your Mobile Data

Everything you do on the computer uses your phone's data bundle. Uploading or downloading large files (especially PDF documents) can quickly drain a 1GB bundle. Switch off hotspot immediately when done.

Incognito Mode — Private Browsing

Incognito mode opens a private window where Chrome does not save your browsing history, cookies, or form data. When you close the incognito window, everything is erased.

What Incognito DOES Do
  • Hides your activity from other users of the same computer
  • Does not save your browsing history
  • Logs you out of all accounts when you close the window
  • Useful for logging into someone else's account without mixing cookies
What Incognito Does NOT Do
  • Does NOT hide your activity from your internet provider
  • Does NOT make you anonymous online
  • Does NOT protect you from viruses or phishing
  • Your employer or cybercafé owner can still see what sites you visited
Use Incognito at the Cybercafé

At a cybercafé, always use Incognito mode for government portals and email. Press Ctrl+Shift+N. When you close the incognito window, your login details are gone — the next customer cannot access your accounts.

Downloading Files from the Internet

How to Download a File
  1. 1 Find the file you want to download — click the download link or button
  2. 2 A download bar appears at the bottom of Chrome — or a "Save As" dialog box opens
  3. 3 Choose where to save the file (e.g., Desktop or Documents folder)
  4. 4 Click Save — the file downloads
  5. 5 When complete, click Open file or go to the Downloads folder
  6. 6 Press Ctrl+J to see all recent downloads in Chrome
Download Safety Rules
  • Only download from websites you trust (look for HTTPS and a padlock)
  • Never download .exe files from unknown websites — these install software, possibly viruses
  • Be careful with "Download" buttons on unfamiliar sites — some are fake ads that install malware
  • PDFs and Word documents are generally safe but always scan with antivirus if unsure

Gmail & Email — Complete Guide

What Is Email and Why Does It Matter?

Email (Electronic Mail) is the most widely used professional communication tool in the world. In Kenya, you need an email address for:

Email Is Required For
  • Registering on eCitizen, KRA, HELB, KUCCPS
  • Applying for jobs (sending your CV)
  • University applications and admissions
  • Receiving PIN certificates and government documents
  • Banking and M-Pesa statement requests
  • Professional communication with employers and clients
Structure of an Email Address
faithachieng@gmail.com
  • faithachieng — your unique username
  • @ — the "at" symbol — separates username from provider
  • gmail — the email provider (Google)
  • .com — the domain type

Creating a Gmail Account — Step by Step

Gmail (from Google) is the most widely used email in Kenya. It is free, reliable, and integrates with Google Drive, Docs, and YouTube.

Gmail Account Creation
  1. 1 Open Chrome → go to www.gmail.com
  2. 2 Click "Create account" → choose "For my personal use"
  3. 3 Enter your First name and Last name → click Next
  4. 4 Enter your Date of birth and Gender → click Next
  5. 5 Choose a Gmail address from the suggestions, or create your own (e.g., faithachieng2026@gmail.com)
  6. 6 Create a strong password (8+ characters, mix of letters, numbers, symbols) → confirm it → Next
  7. 7 Add a phone number (strongly recommended — used for account recovery)
  8. 8 Enter the verification code sent to your phone via SMS
  9. 9 Add a recovery email if you have one (a friend or family member's email)
  10. 10 Review the privacy settings → click "I Agree"
  11. 11 Your Gmail inbox opens — write down your email address and password!
Choosing a Good Gmail Address
  • Use your real name: faithachieng@gmail.com — professional
  • Add a number if taken: faithachieng2026@gmail.com
  • Avoid nicknames for professional use: hotgirl254@gmail.com — unprofessional
  • Once created, you cannot change a Gmail address — choose carefully

Attaching Files to an Email

Attaching a file means including a document, image, or PDF with your email. The recipient can download and open it. This is how you send your CV, certificates, forms, and photos.

How to Attach a File
  1. 1 Click Compose to start a new email
  2. 2 Click the Paperclip icon (📎) at the bottom of the compose window
  3. 3 A file browser opens — navigate to where your file is saved
  4. 4 Click the file to select it → click Open
  5. 5 Wait for the file to upload — a progress bar shows. Large files take longer.
  6. 6 The attachment appears as a small icon below the subject line
  7. 7 Finish writing your email → click Send
Gmail File Size Limits
  • Maximum attachment size: 25MB per email
  • For larger files: use Google Drive — Gmail will automatically share a link instead of attaching
  • Compress PDFs and images first if they are too large (use ilovepdf.com and tinypng.com)
  • If you mention "please find attached" in your email, always check the attachment is actually there before sending!

Replying and Forwarding Emails

Reply

Sends your response back to the person who emailed you. The original message is included below for context.

  1. Open the email → click Reply (or press R)
  2. Type your response
  3. Click Send

Reply All — sends to everyone in the original email, not just the sender. Use carefully!

Forward

Sends the email to a different person. The original message and attachments are included.

  1. Open the email → click Forward (or press F)
  2. Enter the new recipient's email address in the To field
  3. Add your own note if needed
  4. Click Send

Section 6.6

Email Etiquette — Write Professionally

Why Email Etiquette Matters

An email is often the first impression you make on an employer, teacher, or government official. A poorly written email can cost you a job opportunity. A well-written one opens doors. Learn the rules and apply them every time.

Professional Email Habits
  • Always write a clear, specific Subject line
  • Begin with a proper greeting: "Dear Mr. Kamau" or "Dear Hiring Manager"
  • Use polite, formal language throughout
  • Keep the email concise — say what you need to say in as few words as possible
  • End with a polite closing: "Yours faithfully" or "Kind regards"
  • Sign off with your full name, phone number, and email
  • Proofread before sending — check spelling and grammar
  • If you promise an attachment, check it is actually attached!
Email Mistakes to Avoid
  • Sending without a Subject line — recipient doesn't know what the email is about
  • Using ALL CAPITAL LETTERS — it reads as shouting
  • Using slang or texting language: "hae, hw r u?" — unprofessional
  • Very long, unfocused emails — nobody reads them
  • Forgetting to attach the file you mentioned
  • Replying All when only the sender needs to see your reply
  • Sharing confidential documents in unencrypted emails
  • Using an unprofessional email address for formal communication

Phishing — The Most Common Online Attack

Phishing is when fraudsters send fake emails, SMS messages, or create fake websites that look exactly like the real ones — to steal your username, password, money, or personal information. It is the number one way Kenyans lose money online.

⚠️ FAKE PHISHING EMAIL — FOR LEARNING ONLY
From: kra-tax-refund@gmail.com (NOT the real KRA)
🚨 URGENT: Your KRA Tax Refund of Ksh 45,000 is Ready!
Dear Taxpayer,

Your KRA tax refund of KSH 45,000 has been approved and is awaiting collection. Due to system upgrade, you must verify your account within 24 HOURS or lose your refund.

Click the link below to claim your refund now:
🔗 Claim Refund — kra-taxrefund-kenya.com
© 2026 Kenya Revenue Authority • Nairobi
Red Flags in This Email
  • Sender email is kra-tax-refund@gmail.com — the real KRA uses @kra.go.ke
  • Creates false urgency — "claim within 24 HOURS" — pressure tactics are a scam sign
  • Link goes to kra-taxrefund-kenya.com — NOT the real itax.kra.go.ke
  • Promises money you did not expect — if it sounds too good, it is a scam
  • Uses CAPITAL LETTERS and 🚨 emojis to create panic

How to Identify Real vs Fake Websites

Real Government Websites
  • URL ends in .go.ke (e.g., ecitizen.go.ke)
  • Padlock icon (🔒) visible in address bar
  • Address starts with https://
  • No urgent pop-ups demanding your password
  • Professional design with no spelling errors
Fake / Suspicious Websites
  • URL looks similar but different: ecitizin.go.ke or ecitizen-kenya.com
  • No padlock icon or shows "Not Secure"
  • Immediate pop-up asking for password or phone number
  • Poor grammar and spelling mistakes
  • Promises of free money, prizes, or jobs

Other Online Threats — Know Them All

Safe Browsing Rules — Follow These Always

Always Do These
  • Check for https:// and the padlock before entering passwords
  • Log out of all accounts before leaving a public computer
  • Use Incognito mode at cybercafés for government portals
  • Use a different strong password for each important account
  • Keep your browser and antivirus up to date
  • Verify website URLs by typing them manually — do not click links from SMS
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on Gmail and other accounts
Never Do These
  • Never share your password with anyone — not even a cybercafé attendant
  • Never use the same password for all accounts
  • Never click links in unsolicited emails or SMS messages
  • Never enter your M-Pesa PIN or banking details on a website you didn't navigate to manually
  • Never save passwords in a shared or public computer's browser
  • Never give your email password to anyone claiming to be from Google or Gmail
  • Never download software from pop-up windows

Digital Citizenship — Being Responsible Online

The internet is a public space. What you say and do online affects real people. Being a good digital citizen means being as responsible online as you are in real life.

Good Digital Citizen
  • Think before you post — would you say this to their face?
  • Share only accurate information — verify before sharing on WhatsApp
  • Respect others' privacy — do not share people's photos without permission
  • Report cyberbullying and harmful content
  • Give credit when sharing someone else's work
  • Obey copyright — don't download and sell other people's content
Legal Consequences in Kenya
  • Spreading false information online is a criminal offence under the Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act 2018
  • Cyberbullying and harassment are punishable with fines and jail
  • Hacking into computer systems carries up to 10 years imprisonment

6 Practical Activity 6 — Internet Safety Audit
  1. Review your Gmail account — go to Google Account → Security — check if two-factor authentication is enabled. If not, enable it.
  2. Change your Gmail password to a stronger one: minimum 12 characters, including uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  3. Open your browser → visit www.ecitizen.go.ke — verify the padlock icon and https:// are present
  4. Go to your Gmail inbox → find any suspicious emails → select them → click Report as Spam
  5. Press Ctrl+Shift+Delete in Chrome → clear browsing history and cookies (simulate what to do at a cybercafé)
  6. Log out of all accounts: Gmail → profile icon → Sign Out
