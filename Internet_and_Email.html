Section 6.1

What Is the Internet?

Understanding the Internet

The internet is a massive global network of computers, phones, and servers all connected together. When you visit a website, your computer sends a request through your internet connection, travels to a server somewhere in the world, and the website's information is sent back to your screen — all within seconds.

Think of it like a highway: the internet is the road, websites are the destinations, your browser is your car, and your internet provider (ISP) is the fuel station that keeps you moving.

Using Cybercafés in Kenya

If you don't have home internet, cybercafés are your best option. They charge Ksh 20–50 per hour. Always log out of all accounts before leaving. Bring a USB drive to save your work — don't rely on the café's computer to keep your files.

Section 6.3

Google Search — Finding Anything Online

How Google Search Works

Google searches billions of web pages in under a second to show you the most relevant results. The key to getting good results is using the right keywords — the important words that describe what you want, not complete sentences.

Basic Search Tips

Good Search
  • Use short, specific keywords
  • Include the country if needed
  • Use the most important words first
  • Ignore small words (the, a, is, how)
Avoid This
  • Typing full sentences ("Can you please tell me how to…")
  • Using too many vague words
  • Not being specific enough
  • Forgetting to add "Kenya" for local results

Section 6.4

Creating a Gmail Account

Why Gmail?

Gmail (Google Mail) is Kenya's most widely used email service. It is free, trusted by employers, and required for almost every government portal including eCitizen, KRA iTax, and HELB. Your Gmail address becomes your digital identity — choose it carefully.

Choosing a Professional Email Address
  • Good: john.kamau@gmail.com, j.wanjiku2024@gmail.com, akinyi.professional@gmail.com
  • Avoid: coolboy254@gmail.com, hotgirl_nairobi@gmail.com, sexy_kim@gmail.com
  • Employers judge your email address — use your real name. If your name is taken, add your birth year or middle initial.

Creating a Gmail Account — Step by Step

Create Your Gmail Account
  1. 1 Open Chrome and go to accounts.google.com or search "Create Gmail account"
  2. 2 Click "Create account" → Choose "For my personal use"
  3. 3 Enter your First Name and Last Name
  4. 4 Choose your email address (e.g., jane.akinyi2024@gmail.com). If taken, Google suggests alternatives
  5. 5 Create a strong password (see Section 6.7 for tips) — write it down safely
  6. 6 Enter your phone number (for verification and account recovery)
  7. 7 Enter your date of birth and gender
  8. 8 Google sends an SMS verification code to your phone — enter it
  9. 9 Read and Agree to Google's Terms and Privacy Policy
  10. 10 Your account is created! You are now taken to Gmail
Write Down Your Details!

After creating your account, immediately write down: your full email address, your password, and the recovery phone number. Store this in a safe place. Many people get locked out of their accounts because they forgot their password.

Professional Email Etiquette

✅ Always Do This
  • Start with a proper greeting: Dear Mr. Kamau, or Dear Hiring Manager,
  • State your purpose in the first sentence
  • Keep it concise — 3–5 short paragraphs maximum
  • Proofread before sending
  • Close formally: Sincerely, / Kind regards, / Thank you,
  • Include your full name and phone number in the signature
❌ Avoid This
  • All capitals: I NEED THIS URGENTLY (looks aggressive)
  • Slang or texting language: pliz snd me d form asap
  • Vague subject lines: "Hi" or "Question"
  • Forwarding chain emails and fake news
  • Emailing when angry — wait and re-read first
  • Sending large files over 25MB (compress them first)

Replying and Forwarding Emails

Reply

Respond to the person who sent you the email. Open the email, click "Reply" (or press R). The original message is included below — this is called a thread.

Reply All — responds to everyone who received the original email. Use with caution!

Forward

Send an email you received to a different person. Open the email, click "Forward". You must type the new recipient's address in the "To" field.

Useful for sharing documents or information with a third party.

Before You Hit Send — The 5-Point Check
  • ✅ Is the "To" address correct? (One wrong letter = wrong person)
  • ✅ Is the subject line clear and professional?
  • ✅ Have you proofread for spelling and grammar?
  • ✅ Did you attach the file if you mentioned an attachment?
  • ✅ Is the tone professional and polite?

Organising Your Gmail

As emails pile up, it becomes hard to find important messages. Use Gmail's organisation tools to stay on top of your inbox.

Create Labels (like Folders)

  4. 4 Open any email, click the Label icon (🏷️) and choose your label
  5. 5 That email now appears under your label in the sidebar

Section 6.6

Attachments — Sending and Receiving Files

How to Attach a File to an Email

An attachment is a file (document, photo, PDF) that you include with an email. Common examples: attaching your CV when applying for jobs, or attaching a scanned ID for government applications.

Attaching a File in Gmail
  1. 1 Click "Compose" to write a new email
  2. 2 At the bottom of the compose window, click the paperclip icon 📎
  3. 3 A file explorer window opens — navigate to your file (e.g., on Desktop or USB drive)
  4. 4 Click the file to select it, then click Open
  5. 5 The file appears as a thumbnail below your email — you can see the filename
  6. 6 You can attach multiple files — just click the paperclip icon again
  7. 7 Click Send — the recipient will see the attachment when they open your email
Gmail's 25MB Attachment Limit

Gmail only allows attachments up to 25MB in total. If your files are larger:

  • Compress images using a free tool like tinypng.com
  • For large files, use Google Drive — attach the Drive link instead
  • Compress multiple files into a .zip folder before attaching

Downloading Attachments You Receive

Download an Attachment
  1. 1 Open the email containing the attachment
  2. 2 Scroll to the bottom — you'll see the attached file(s) as icons
  3. 3 Hover over the file — a small download icon ⬇️ appears
  4. 4 Click the download icon — the file downloads to your Downloads folder
  5. 5 Open File Explorer → Downloads to find and open your file
Never Open Suspicious Attachments!
  • Do not open attachments from people you don't know
  • Be wary of .exe, .bat, or .vbs files — these can be viruses
  • Legitimate banks, KRA, and eCitizen never send attachments asking for your password
  • If in doubt, do NOT open the file

Google Drive — Share Large Files

Google Drive gives you 15GB of free cloud storage. Store documents, photos, and files online — accessible from any device. It's especially useful for sharing files too large to email.

Upload to Drive
  1. Go to drive.google.com
  2. Click "+ New" → File Upload
  3. Select your file — it uploads to your Drive
Share from Drive
  1. Right-click the file in Drive
  2. Click "Share"
  3. Enter the recipient's email address
  4. Or click "Copy link" to paste in email
6 Attachments Practice
  1. Open Microsoft Word — create a simple 1-page document with your name and skills
  2. Save it as YourName_Skills.docx on the Desktop
  3. Compose a new email to yourself — attach this Word document
  4. Send it and then open your inbox to confirm it arrived
  5. Download the attachment from the received email
  6. Upload the file to Google Drive (drive.google.com) and copy the share link

Section 6.7

Online Safety, Passwords & Avoiding Scams

Creating Strong Passwords

Your password is the lock on the door to your digital life. A weak password can be cracked in seconds. A strong password takes centuries to crack with a computer.

kenya2024
Very Weak
Nairobi@2024
Moderate
Ng0ma!Safar1&Kib@ra
Very Strong

Rules for a Strong Password

A Strong Password Has…
  • At least 12 characters (the longer the better)
  • UPPERCASE and lowercase letters
  • At least one number (1, 2, 3...)
  • At least one symbol (!@#$%&)
  • No dictionary words on their own
  • Nothing personal (no birthdate, name, or ID number)
Weak Password Examples
  • password (most common worldwide)
  • 123456 or 12345678
  • Your name or birth year
  • Your phone number
  • qwerty or abcdef
  • The word "Kenya" or your town name
Password Creation Trick (Passphrase Method)

Think of a sentence you'll remember: "I moved to Nairobi in 2018 and love ugali!"
Take the first letter of each word: ImtNi2018alu!
This is a 13-character password that's strong AND memorable.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — Your Second Lock

Even a strong password can be stolen. Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) means that to log in, you need two things: your password AND a code sent to your phone. Even if someone steals your password, they cannot log in without your phone.

Step 1

Enter your email and password as usual

Step 2

Google sends a 6-digit code to your phone via SMS

Step 3

Enter the 6-digit code into the login page

Step 4

You are now logged in securely — even if your password was stolen, the hacker cannot access your account

Enable 2FA on Gmail
  1. 1 Go to myaccount.google.com
  2. 2 Click Security in the left menu
  3. 3 Under "How you sign in to Google", click "2-Step Verification"
  4. 4 Click Get started and follow the prompts
  5. 5 Add your phone number — Google will verify it with an SMS code
  6. 6 2FA is now active — your account is much more secure

Recognising Phishing Scams

Phishing is when criminals send fake emails pretending to be a trusted organisation (KRA, Safaricom, your bank) to trick you into giving your password, ID, or money.

🚨 PHISHING EMAIL — FAKE
From: kra-kenya.tax-refund.net
Sent from a suspicious domain (.net, not .go.ke)
Subject: "URGENT: Your KRA tax refund of Ksh 25,000 is waiting"
Asks you to click a link and "verify" your PIN and National ID
Creates urgency: "You have 24 hours or your refund is cancelled"
Poor grammar and spelling
✅ GENUINE KRA EMAIL
From: noreply@kra.go.ke
Sent from official @kra.go.ke domain
Subject: "iTax — Your return has been submitted"
Does NOT ask for your password or PIN by email
Links go to itax.kra.go.ke (official domain)
Professional, correctly spelled language
Common Kenyan Online Scams to Recognise
  • M-Pesa Refund Scam: "You accidentally sent me Ksh 5,000 — please return it." Do not refund strangers. Always verify via your M-Pesa statement.
  • Lottery / Job Win: "You have won Ksh 1,000,000 — pay Ksh 2,000 processing fee." No legitimate lottery asks you to pay first.
  • Fake eCitizen / KRA emails: Always go to the website directly — never click email links to log in.
  • WhatsApp Impersonation: "Hi, this is your cousin — new number. Please send Ksh 3,000 urgently." Call back the original number to verify.
  • Online Selling Fraud: "Send M-Pesa first, I'll deliver the phone." Only pay on delivery for unknown sellers.

HTTPS — The Green Padlock

Before entering any personal information (password, National ID, bank details) on a website, always check for HTTPS in the address bar. The padlock 🔒 means your data is encrypted as it travels to the server.

🔒 HTTPS — Safe

https://www.ecitizen.go.ke

Padlock icon visible. Data is encrypted. Safe to enter passwords, IDs, and personal details.

⚠️ HTTP — Not Secure

http://random-site.com

No padlock or "Not Secure" warning. Do NOT enter passwords, payment details, or personal information.

Safe Internet Habits

Your Online Safety Checklist
  • Use a different, strong password for each important account (Gmail, eCitizen, banking)
  • Never share your password with anyone — not even family or "bank staff"
  • Always log out of accounts on shared computers (cybercafés)
  • Lock your phone with a PIN or fingerprint to protect your email app
  • Keep your computer and phone updated — updates patch security holes
  • Use antivirus software (Windows Defender is built-in and free on Windows)
  • Think before you click: hover over links to see where they really go
  • If something seems too good to be true, it is a scam
7 Online Safety Practice
  1. Check your Gmail password — is it 12+ characters with letters, numbers, and symbols?
  2. Enable 2-Factor Authentication on your Gmail account (myaccount.google.com → Security)
  3. Look at the URL bar for ecitizen.go.ke — confirm the padlock 🔒 is present
  4. Look through your Gmail Spam folder — identify 3 signs of phishing in one email
  5. Write down your email, password, and recovery phone — store in a safe place at home
