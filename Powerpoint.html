Microsoft PowerPoint is the world's leading presentation software. It transforms ideas into visual stories that educate, persuade, and inspire. Every teacher, student, business professional, and community leader in Kenya who can deliver a confident PowerPoint presentation has a significant advantage.

Unlike Word (for reading) or Excel (for calculating), PowerPoint is for showing and telling. It combines text, images, charts, and animations into slides that you present on screen, projector, or print as handouts.

Section 5.2

Creating Slides, Layouts & Organising Your Deck

Adding and Managing Slides

Add a New Slide
  • Home Tab → New Slide button
  • OR press Ctrl+M
  • OR right-click in the Slides Pane → New Slide

Choose a layout when prompted, or change it after.

Duplicate a Slide
  • Right-click the slide in the Slides Pane → Duplicate Slide
  • OR select it and press Ctrl+D

Duplicating keeps the same layout and content as a starting point.

Delete a Slide
  • Right-click the slide thumbnail → Delete Slide
  • OR click the slide → press Delete
Reorder Slides
  • In the Slides Pane: click and drag a slide to its new position
  • In Slide Sorter View (View Tab): drag multiple slides at once for easier reordering

Slide Layouts — Use the Right One Every Time

A layout is a pre-designed template that positions placeholders (title, content, images) on the slide. Always choose the layout that matches your content — don't fight the slide by repositioning everything manually.

Section 5.3

Design, Themes & Professional Slide Principles

Applying Themes

A Theme automatically applies a coordinated set of colours, fonts, backgrounds, and effects to your entire presentation. It is the fastest way to make a presentation look professional and consistent.

How to Apply a Theme
  1. 1 Go to the Design Tab
  2. 2 Browse the Themes gallery — hover over each to preview
  3. 3 Click a theme to apply it to all slides
  4. 4 Use Variants (right side of gallery) to choose a colour variation
Change Background Colour
  1. Design Tab → Format Background
  2. Choose: Solid Fill, Gradient, Picture, Pattern
  3. Click Apply to All for all slides, or leave it for just the current slide
Change Slide Size
  • Widescreen (16:9) — modern screens and projectors. This is the default.
  • Standard (4:3) — older projectors

Design Tab → Slide Size

The Golden Rules of Good Slide Design

Do These — Professional Slides
  • One idea per slide — don't crowd multiple topics
  • Max 6 words per line — force yourself to be brief
  • Max 5–6 bullet points per slide
  • Use images more than text — a picture communicates instantly
  • High contrast — dark text on light background or vice versa
  • Consistent font and colour scheme throughout
  • Large text — minimum 20pt so back row can read it
  • Use slide numbers — helps audience reference specific slides
Avoid These — Amateur Mistakes
  • Copying entire paragraphs from a document onto a slide
  • Font size below 18pt — impossible to read on screen
  • More than 3 different font styles on one slide
  • Neon colours on white or bright backgrounds
  • Clip art from the 1990s — use real, relevant photos
  • Spelling mistakes — always run Spell Check (Review Tab)
  • Talking to the screen instead of the audience
  • Putting everything on slides — your slides support you, not replace you
The 5/5/5 Rule for Presentations

No more than 5 words per line · No more than 5 lines per slide · No more than 5 text-heavy slides in a row. The audience listens to you — the slides just support your key points.

3 Practical Activity 3 — Design Your Presentation
  1. Open your presentation → go to Design Tab → apply a professional theme
  2. Try 3 different theme Variants → choose the one that looks cleanest
  3. Change the background of Slide 1 only to a dark colour for impact
  4. Review all slides — ensure all titles are size 36pt or larger
  5. Run Spelling & Grammar check (Review Tab) and fix any errors
  6. Check: does each slide have ONE clear idea? Are bullets concise?
  7. Save your presentation

Section 5.4

Inserting Images, Shapes, Tables & Charts

Inserting and Formatting Images

Insert an Image from Your Computer
  1. 1 Click the slide where you want the image
  2. 2 Go to Insert Tab → Pictures → This Device
  3. 3 Browse and select your image file → click Insert
  4. 4 The image appears on the slide — resize using the corner handles
  5. 5 Drag the image to position it correctly

After Inserting — Useful Image Tools (Picture Format Tab)

  • Crop: Remove unwanted edges — drag the crop handles → press Enter
  • Picture Styles: Apply a frame, shadow, or rounded corner effect with one click
  • Corrections: Adjust brightness and contrast
  • Remove Background: PowerPoint can automatically remove the background from a photo
  • Artistic Effects: Apply painterly or sketch effects to photos
Always Resize from Corners, Never from Sides

Dragging the side handles stretches the image and distorts it. Always drag from the corner handles to resize proportionally. Hold Shift while dragging to lock the aspect ratio perfectly.

Inserting Shapes and Text Boxes

Insert a Shape
  1. Insert Tab → Shapes
  2. Click a shape (rectangle, circle, arrow, callout…)
  3. Click and drag on the slide to draw it
  4. Use Shape Format Tab to change fill colour, outline, and effects
  5. Right-click → Add Text to type inside the shape
Insert a Text Box
  1. Insert Tab → Text Box
  2. Click and drag on the slide to create the text area
  3. Type your text
  4. Resize and move as needed

Use text boxes when you want text outside the standard layout placeholders.

Aligning Multiple Objects Perfectly

Select multiple shapes (click first, then Shift+click others) → Shape Format Tab → Arrange → Align. Choose Align Left, Centre, Right, Top, Middle, or Bottom to align them precisely without guessing.

Inserting Charts

Charts in PowerPoint work exactly like in Excel. The data is edited in a mini Excel spreadsheet that opens when you insert the chart.

Insert a Chart
  1. 1 Go to Insert Tab → Chart
  2. 2 Choose your chart type (Column, Bar, Line, Pie) → click OK
  3. 3 A mini Excel spreadsheet opens with sample data
  4. 4 Replace the sample data with your own data
  5. 5 Close the spreadsheet — your chart updates automatically
  6. 6 Click the chart → use the Chart Design Tab to add titles, data labels, and styles

Inserting Tables

Insert a Table
  1. 1 Insert Tab → Table
  2. 2 Drag over the grid to select rows × columns (e.g., 4 columns × 5 rows)
  3. 3 Type data into cells — press Tab to move between cells
  4. 4 Use Table Design Tab to apply a table style
Keep Tables Simple on Slides

Slides are not documents. A table with more than 5 columns and 6 rows is too large for a slide — the audience can't read it. Use tables for simple comparisons only.

4 Practical Activity 4 — Visual Slide Design
  1. On Slide 2, insert an image from your computer that is relevant to your topic
  2. Crop the image to remove unwanted edges, apply a Picture Style
  3. Draw a rounded rectangle shape → change fill to your theme accent colour → add text inside it as a caption
  4. On Slide 3, insert a Pie Chart showing time spent daily: Study 4hrs, Sleep 8hrs, Exercise 1hr, Leisure 3hrs, Work 8hrs
  5. Add Data Labels with percentages to the chart
  6. On Slide 4, insert a 3×4 table comparing 3 digital skills you've learned
  7. Save your presentation

Section 5.5

Slide Transitions — The Effect Between Slides

What Is a Transition?

A transition is the visual effect that plays when you move from one slide to the next during your presentation. You see transitions every day — news programmes use them, websites use them, and videos use them. Used well, transitions make a presentation feel polished and professional. Used badly, they become distracting.

The Professional's Transition Rule

In business, education, and formal settings: use Fade or Push only — apply to all slides — duration 0.5 to 0.8 seconds. Simple, elegant, never distracting. Save the flashy effects for birthday presentations!

Applying and Customising Transitions

Add a Transition
  1. 1 Click the slide you want to add a transition to (in the Slides Pane)
  2. 2 Go to the Transitions Tab
  3. 3 Click a transition from the gallery (hover to preview)
  4. 4 Click Effect Options to change the direction (From Left, From Right, etc.)
  5. 5 Set Duration — recommended: 0.50–0.80 seconds for professional look
  6. 6 Add a Sound if appropriate (usually leave as [No Sound])
  7. 7 Click Apply To All to use the same transition on every slide
Advance Slide: On Click

Default — the slide advances only when you click the mouse or press an arrow key. Best for presentations where you control the pace.

Advance Slide: After Time

Slide advances automatically after a set number of seconds. Under Timing group → check "After" → set seconds. Best for kiosk or self-running presentations.

5 Practical Activity 5 — Apply Transitions
  1. Go to the Transitions Tab
  2. Apply Fade to Slide 1 — set duration to 0.70 seconds
  3. Apply Push (from Right) to Slides 2–4
  4. Apply Cut (no transition) to the final Thank You slide
  5. Ensure all transitions have No Sound
  6. Press F5 to preview the full slideshow — check transitions feel smooth
  7. Save your presentation

Section 5.6

Animations — Bringing Objects to Life

Transitions vs Animations — Know the Difference

Transition

The effect that plays when you move from one slide to another.

Example: Slide 1 fades out, Slide 2 fades in.

Applied to: the entire slide

Animation

The effect applied to a specific object on a slide — text box, image, shape, or chart.

Example: A bullet point flies in from the left when you click.

Applied to: individual objects

The Four Types of Animation

Entrance — Object appears
Appear — instantly visible
Fade — gently fades in
Fly In — slides in from edge
Zoom — grows from centre
Float In — drifts in gently
Wipe — reveals like a curtain

Pulse — grows and shrinks
Spin — rotates
Bold Flash — bolds briefly

Disappear — instantly gone
Fade Out — gently fades away
Fly Out — slides off screen

Loops — circular path

Applying and Controlling Animations

Add an Animation to an Object
  1. 1 Click the object you want to animate (text box, image, shape, or chart)
  2. 2 Go to the Animations Tab
  3. 3 Click an animation from the gallery (Entrance: Fade, Fly In, Appear are best)
  4. 4 Click Effect Options to change direction (From Left, From Bottom, etc.)
  5. 5 Set the Start option in the Timing group
  6. 6 Set Duration (0.50–1.00 seconds recommended)
  7. 7 Set Delay if you want a pause before the animation starts

Animation Start Options — Critical to Understand

Start OptionWhen It PlaysBest For
On ClickOnly when you click the mouse or press an arrow keyRevealing bullet points one at a time — you control the pace
With PreviousPlays at the same time as the animation before itMultiple objects appearing simultaneously
After PreviousPlays automatically after the previous animation finishesObjects appearing in sequence without clicking

The Animation Pane — Your Control Centre

The Animation Pane shows a list of all animations on the current slide. Open it with Animations Tab → Animation Pane.

  • Reorder animations: drag them up or down in the pane to change the sequence
  • See timing: a timeline bar shows each animation's duration and delay visually
  • Edit an animation: double-click it in the pane to open detailed settings
  • Remove an animation: select it in the pane → press Delete
  • Preview animations: click the Play button at the top of the Animation Pane
Animation Mistakes That Kill Presentations
  • Animating every single object on every slide — exhausting for the audience
  • Using spinning, bouncing, or swivelling effects in professional settings
  • Animations that take over 1.5 seconds — too slow, audience gets bored
  • Not previewing — always press Shift+F5 to test from the current slide
6 Practical Activity 6 — Animate Your Slides
  1. Go to Slide 1 — click the title text box
  2. Apply Fade animation → Start: After Previous → Duration: 0.8s
  3. Click the subtitle → apply Fly In (From Bottom) → Start: After Previous → Delay: 0.5s
  4. Go to Slide 2 — click the content text box (bullet points)
  5. Apply Appear → Start: On Click → this reveals each bullet on a separate click
  6. Open the Animation Pane — check the order of all animations
  7. On Slide 3, animate the chart to Zoom → Start: On Click
  8. Press Shift+F5 to preview from current slide — check timing feels natural
  9. Save your presentation

Section 5.7

Delivering Your Slideshow & Sharing Your Work

Running the Slide Show

Start the Presentation
  • Press F5 — starts from Slide 1
  • Press Shift+F5 — starts from current slide
  • Slide Show Tab → From Beginning
Keyboard During Slideshow
  • → / ↓ / Space / Click — Next slide
  • ← / ↑ — Previous slide
  • B — Black screen (pause)
  • W — White screen
  • Number + Enter — Jump to slide 3, 7, etc.
  • Esc — End the slideshow
Laser Pointer During Presentation

During a slideshow, hold Ctrl and click to activate the laser pointer. Use it to point to specific parts of the slide without touching the screen. Right-click during the slideshow to access pen, highlighter, and other tools.

Presenter View — Your Secret Weapon

Presenter View is a split-screen mode where your screen shows the current slide, your notes, the next slide, and a timer — while the audience screen shows only the current slide.

Enable Presenter View

  2. 2 Go to Slide Show Tab → Monitors group
  3. 3 Check "Use Presenter View"
  4. 4 Start the slide show — your screen shows Presenter View, projector shows slides
Rehearse Timings

Slide Show Tab → Rehearse Timings records how long you spend on each slide during practice. PowerPoint saves these timings and can use them to automatically advance slides during the real presentation. Use this to ensure your presentation fits in your allocated time.

Printing Slides and Handouts

Print Your Presentation
  1. 1 Press Ctrl+P (or File → Print)
  2. 2 Choose your Printer
  3. 3 Choose Print Layout:
    Full Page Slides — 1 slide per page (for presenting)
    Handouts (2 per page) — 2 slides per page (audience notes)
    Handouts (3 per page) — 3 slides with lines for notes
    Notes Pages — slide + speaker notes below
  4. 4 Choose Colour or Greyscale (greyscale saves ink)
  5. 5 Click Print

Always Send CVs and Formal Presentations as PDF

When emailing your presentation to an employer, teacher, or client, always export as PDF first. This prevents fonts from shifting, animations from breaking, and layout changes on the recipient's computer.

The 5-Slide Structure — How to Plan Any Presentation

Good presentations are planned before they are built. Use this 5-slide structure for any topic — school, business, or community.

1

Title Slide — Who, What, When

Your name, presentation title, date, and institution or company. Make a visual impact — use a strong image or bold colour.

Layout: Title Slide
2

Introduction / The Problem

What is this about? Why does it matter? Hook the audience in the first 30 seconds. Use a surprising fact, question, or image.

Layout: Title and Content
3

Main Content — Evidence / Details

Your 3–5 key points, data, examples, or steps. Use bullet points, a chart, table, or image. One idea per slide if needed.

Layout: Title and Content / Two Content
4

Conclusion / Call to Action

Summarise the key message. What do you want the audience to do or remember? Make the action clear and specific.

Layout: Title and Content
5

Thank You / Q&A / Contact

Thank the audience. Invite questions. Leave your contact details — email, phone. This slide stays on screen during the Q&A.

Layout: Title Slide or Blank
